I wear many hats: biostatistician, geographer, traveler, linguist, translator of Swahili, anthropologist, ecologist, epidemiologist, professional gamelan musician, quizbowl editor, and more. I graduated from Duke University in 2024 with a B.S. in Health and Medical Geography of Human–Environmental Interactions, and I'm an incoming MD-PhD student.

## Research

My research focuses on how disease transmission — especially of vector-borne neglected tropical diseases — is driven by environmental processes, human–environment interactions, and social context. I use GIS, network modeling, and biostatistical methods to study these questions, with field and analytical experience spanning Madagascar, Central Africa, the Venezuelan Amazon, and Durham, NC.

I've worked as a biostatistician at the David Lab (Duke School of Medicine), studying diet through DNA metabarcoding; as a researcher at the Nunn Lab, modeling disease diffusion in Madagascar using transportation network analysis; and as a volunteer researcher at the NIH, studying the effects of residential segregation on mental health.

## Languages

I speak Tamil and Telugu natively, along with Spanish, French, Hindi, Portuguese, Egyptian Arabic, Italian, German, and others. I also work with Swahili as a translator and have a deep interest in the world's literary traditions — I maintain a [reading list](/blog/) spanning from the *Exaltation of Inanna* (c. 2300 BCE) to the present, across dozens of languages and traditions.

This is a space for my academic work, writing, and projects. The blog will feature data visualizations, mapping projects, linguistic explorations, and whatever else I find interesting enough to share.
